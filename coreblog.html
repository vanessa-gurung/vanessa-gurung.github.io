1. Using the evidence gathered, describe your core personal values.

Explain a situation where you have had an ethical decision to make. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and the consequences of your action.

Working in public libraries for over five years has put me in quite a few predicaments where ethical decisions had to be made. Most of them occurred when I worked at Central City Library, where many rough sleepers would inhabit. There was one instance on a shift where I caught a regular rough sleeper taking some newspaper from our collection and scrunching the pages to put under his sweater to keep himself warm. I mean, ideally, he shouldn’t have been using what would be considered ‘council property,’ but I wasn't going to rat him out over something that wouldn’t have had to happen if ‘the system’ didn’t fail him. Had I narked, a security incident report would’ve been written up, which are done to keep a record of trouble patrons. After a certain amount of ‘offences,’ they’ll be trespassed from the library. That played into my decision of not reporting anything, despite that being the correct protocol. Instead, I just told him we had heaps of scrap paper, which he could ask for at the desk at any time. I think that was the best outcome for all parties involved– he learnt he could just ask for paper, I didn’t have to write a report and the newspapers remained undamaged (I mean not the best, cause ideally he wouldn't have to do this at all, but you know what I mean). I guess the main value that informed my decision was compassion, since I knew he relied on library resources and I didn’t want him kicked out. But also, it just seemed like a no brainer that someone’s basic needs are more important than minor council property?

Describe how your culture has influenced your values and identity.

Being a second generation immigrant from Nepal, growing up in Auckland, I had a bit of a hard time navigating my identity. It was pretty frustrating for the most part because it seemed split a lot of the time – who I was at home vs who I was outside of. It’s kind of silly thinking about it in retrospect, because it was all me, but it felt like two different people when I was younger. I had a pretty sheltered upbringing and was raised to be a helpful, studious daughter (classic Asian expectations). And for the most part I was, but my interests differed significantly from my parents. They wanted me to do engineering and I did for a year, but I dropped out because I didn’t really like anything about it. I ended up going to art school, where I felt much more ‘myself.’ I guess to some degree, that’s what art school’s largely about, being your ‘authentic self.’ It was quite a shift in mentality, going from my collectivist mindset at home to an extreme individualistic mindset at art school, but I think the latter made me realise the importance of authenticity. While the former taught me to value family, loyalty, dependability and tolerance. I think above all though, practicing compassion has always been something I’ve tried to live by, cause it’s important in every facet of life -work, home, anywhere else. Most people are having a stink time, we should be understanding and nice to one another.

Emotional Intelligence

What is Emotional Intelligence?

EQ is a person’s ability to recognize emotions in oneself and others. It is also using this awareness to regulate emotions and make good decisions.

How is it different to IQ

IQ is a measurement of different qualities that make up our intelligence, such as problem solving skills, analytical thinking, short term memory and spatial recognition. It is used to measure a person’s inherent intelligence, independent from any facts or knowledge they’ve gained over the course of their life. Whereas EQ is largely gained from life experiences and can be improved through self-awareness and personal development training.

Why is it important?

Emotions help us understand and relate to others, learn new things and have a significant influence on our actions and they help us to lead fulfilled lives. Emotions can be difficult to manage though, so having EQ is necessary to harness our emotions in positive way. It allows us to effectively communicate with others and to be able to relate to them on a deeper level. This is beneficial for any context really, but particularly in the workplace where one has to work in a team or manage people.



Neuroplasticity & Growth Mindset

1. Research Neuroplasticity

Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people.

Neuroplasticity describes how the brain can change throughout life by adapting, learning and recovering. Just by knowing that the brain is malleable, people can perform better at whatever task they’re doing. Through neuroplasticity, people can recover from brain events and injuries, enhance memory abilities, various cognitive abilities and learn more effectively.

Discuss how you might engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit.

I think I’ve always been prone to scepticism and pessimism. My default is to always just assume maybe not the worst, but like not much. This is what I’ve believed to be a coping mechanism for most of my adult life, because having low expectations means rarely getting disappointed, but that’s probably not a healthy way to think constantly??? I’m pretty keen to like ... think about things more positively (writing that was embarrassing for me), which I think the principles of neuroplasticity could definitely help with. Like telling myself “I’m the shit!” every day – if I do it enough, maybe I will genuinely think it one day?!

Creating art, which boosts introspection, memory, attention and focus. I can attest to this! When I used to draw diary comics regularly, I noticed I was way more observant of things and the practice made my memory a lot better in a couple of ways – I would make sure to take mental notes of the occurrence that I would want to draw later, and the muscle memory in my hand would also improve. Because they were comics about me and my experiences, I’d be doing a lot of introspection throughout the process as well. I should really get back into it ...
Other ways include but are not limited to: meditation/yoga, reading and learning an instrument.
